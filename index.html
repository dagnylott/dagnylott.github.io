PROJECTS

Matlab

In the spring, the Washington Post released a model that measured the effect of social distancing on the spread of COVID-19. Although their model did not have any scientific importance, I thought it did a great job of conveying the enormous impact social distancing can have on flattening the curve. My model mimicked the Post's ball simulation and graphs. The Matlab skills that I used to do this were: modeling particle dynamics, graphics, real-time animation, user interaction, and data analysis. I also learned how to create buttons and callback functions.

The main difference between my model and the Washington Post's is that the user can adjust two parameters, giving the user a lot more information and the ability to recreate a new scenario each time. For context, a Gallup poll at the end of April reported that approximately 80% of the U.S. population were following the social distancing guidlines. One could have included additional testing parameters such as the number of tests administered, when the country started testing, and whether testing was random or targeted.

Laser Cutting

I designed this rocking horse when I started to learn SolidWorks in my Introduction to Mechanical Design course. I used a laser cutter to cut clear acrylic for the horse's body and MDF for the base. The horse has three types of joints: press fittings, single-lap joints, and two t-nut/tab & slot connections.

SolidWorks

In the same course, I used SolidWorks to CAD a model of a soap dispenser. I took apart the dispenser and recorded basic measurements to ensure the proportions were right. I also incorporated the motion of the spring in my model (the video shown on the left is a SolidWorks animation).

This class was extremely helpful when it came to learning the basics of SolidWorks, Laser Cutting, and 3D printing. Since then, I have gained a lot of experience with SolidWorks during my 16-week internship at Temple Allen Industries and I feel comfortable with large assembly models. The projects that I completed at my internship have not been released to the public yet so they won't be showcased here, but I would love to talk about them in some capacity.

Water Show

In the summer of 2017 and 2018 I directed my local pool's Water Show – an annual theatrical play in and around the pool. Yes, that means wet costumes, choreographed scenes with kickboards and noodles, splashing, and of course more splashing! The first show was Robin Hood followed by Mulan: both experiences were as equally challenging as they were rewarding.

I typically opened sign-ups for pool members in mid-June and started developing the choreographed scenes. In early July, I began leading multiple practices a week (one for each group of 8-10 kids) and if the kids were lucky we would wrap up the day with popsicles. As practices continued, I wrote the script, selected a playlist, designed costumes, purchased props, constructed the set, and got lifeguards to join the cast. All of these components came together in the last week of July for two dress rehearsals and the final show.

At the time, I knew I was gaining leadership experience but it felt like none of the skills I was learning would ever help me in a future engineering job. I was wrong – it required the same type of problem solving. I liked to envision the final stage (the product) with the characters acting out the scenes (the assembly steps).

The editor of my local newspaper, The Swarthmorean, liked my graduation photos and decided to publish them. He asked if I would like to run their Floating Photographer column, which had been my favorite section to read in the newspaper so I quickly jumped on the offer. Usually, one of us would choose a question and then I would walk around the town center to collect responses. I really enjoyed talking to people, of all ages and backgrounds, who I would not have met otherwise. I haven't been doing any "official" photography recently but I'm planning on pitching a similar column to the UPenn newspaper when I return.
